# Michael Nielsen's 8 Principles of Writing Craft

Use this rubric when analyzing writing. Each principle has specific signals to look for.

## 1. Establish a Single, Sharp Purpose

**What to check:**
- Can you state the piece's purpose in one sentence?
- Does every paragraph serve that purpose?
- Are there tangents or "discursive disease" — ideas that are good but don't belong here?

**Strong:** Every section drives toward a single, clear thesis. No tangents.
**Needs Work:** Purpose is identifiable but some sections drift or include loosely related material.
**Weak:** Purpose is vague, shifts mid-piece, or multiple competing ideas fight for attention.

**Common violations:**
- "While we're on the topic of X, let's also discuss Y..."
- Sections that could be removed without affecting the main argument
- Introductions that promise one thing, bodies that deliver another

## 2. Apply Occam's Razor for Writing

**What to check:**
- Could any sentence be shorter without losing meaning?
- Are there complex words where simple ones would work? (e.g., "utilize" → "use", "facilitate" → "help")
- Does the writer use "expert" language that sounds authoritative but adds nothing?

**Strong:** Lean prose. Every word earns its place. Simple vocabulary chosen deliberately.
**Needs Work:** Some bloat — redundant phrases, unnecessarily complex words, or padded sentences.
**Weak:** Verbose throughout. Sentences routinely twice as long as they need to be.

**Common violations:**
- "In order to" → "to"
- "Due to the fact that" → "because"
- "It is important to note that" → cut entirely
- "Leverage", "utilize", "facilitate", "methodology" when simpler words work
- Restating the same idea in different words within a paragraph

## 3. Eliminate Danger Words

**What to check:**
- Phrases that tell the reader how to feel: "It's interesting to consider...", "Remarkably...", "Fascinatingly..."
- Generic intensifiers: "very", "quite", "extremely", "highly", "incredibly"
- Hedging that weakens claims: "somewhat", "arguably", "perhaps it could be said that"

**Strong:** No feeling-instructions. Specific, strong words replace vague intensifiers.
**Needs Work:** A few danger words slip through. Some hedging where conviction would serve better.
**Weak:** Littered with "very", "interesting", "quite", and other dead weight.

**Danger word inventory** (flag every instance):
- very, quite, extremely, highly, incredibly, remarkably
- interesting, fascinating, noteworthy, notable
- it's worth noting, it should be noted, importantly
- arguably, somewhat, rather, fairly

**Rewrite pattern:** Replace "very [adjective]" with a single, precise word. "Very tired" → "exhausted". "Very important" → "critical". If no precise word exists, cut the intensifier.

## 4. Craft Striking Openings

**What to check:**
- Does the first sentence make you want to read the second?
- Is there a long preamble before the piece gets to the point?
- Does the opening set up a problem, tension, or surprise?

**Strong:** The opening grabs attention immediately. No throat-clearing. Drops the reader into the action or a provocation.
**Needs Work:** Opening is functional but conventional. Gets to the point within a paragraph.
**Weak:** Long preamble, generic context-setting, or "In this article, we will discuss..."

**Common violations:**
- "Since the beginning of time..." or any historical sweep opening
- "In this post/article/essay, I will..."
- "X is an important topic because..."
- Multiple paragraphs of background before the actual argument starts

**Test:** Cover the first paragraph. Does the piece work better starting from paragraph two? If yes, cut paragraph one.

## 5. Wage War on the Conventional Story

**What to check:**
- Does the piece offer a fresh angle or just restate common knowledge?
- Are metaphors and descriptions original or cliched?
- Do explanations start from concrete observation rather than abstract generalization?

**Strong:** Fresh perspective. Original metaphors grounded in specific observation. The reader learns something they haven't read elsewhere.
**Needs Work:** Some original ideas mixed with conventional framing. A few cliches.
**Weak:** Reads like it could have been written by anyone. Stock phrases, expected metaphors, no surprises.

**Common violations:**
- "X is like a double-edged sword"
- "At the end of the day"
- "The elephant in the room"
- Explanations that match the first Google result for the topic
- Descriptions that use expected adjectives (a "bustling" city, a "sprawling" codebase)

**Rewrite pattern:** Replace abstract descriptions with concrete, specific observations. Instead of "the city was vibrant," describe what you actually see, hear, or notice.

## 6. Structure for Engagement

**What to check:**
- Does the piece use the micro-to-macro pattern? (specific example → broad question → unexpected answer)
- Is it structured with the reader's psychology in mind?
- Does each section create enough curiosity to pull the reader forward?

**Strong:** Opens with a concrete micro-story or example, escalates to broader implications, delivers an unexpected insight. Reader motivation is managed throughout.
**Needs Work:** Logical structure but doesn't manage reader curiosity. Information presented but not dramatized.
**Weak:** Flat structure. List of facts or arguments with no narrative arc. Reader has no reason to keep going.

**Structural patterns to look for:**
- Does each section end with an implicit "and here's why that matters" that pulls into the next?
- Are examples placed before abstractions (good) or after (less engaging)?
- Is there a payoff that rewards the reader for reading to the end?

## 7. Write the Truth

**What to check:**
- Does every sentence feel honest and precise, or are some vague approximations?
- Are there sentences that sound good but don't quite mean anything?
- Could the writer defend every claim if challenged?

**Strong:** Every sentence is precise and defensible. No hand-waving. Claims are specific enough to be falsifiable.
**Needs Work:** Mostly honest but some sentences are vague or decorative rather than substantive.
**Weak:** Contains "applause lines" — sentences designed to sound impressive rather than convey truth. Vague generalizations passed off as insight.

**Common violations:**
- "X will revolutionize Y" — unsubstantiated prediction
- "Everyone knows that..." — appeal to authority of the crowd
- Sentences that sound wise but dissolve under scrutiny
- Hedged claims that avoid committing to anything ("it could potentially perhaps...")

**Test:** Read each sentence and ask: "Is this the sharpest, truest version of this idea? Do I believe this?"

## 8. Generate Impactful Titles

**What to check:**
- Does the title juxtapose unexpected concepts?
- Do section headers use active verbs or brilliantly concise terms?
- Are headers descriptive and momentum-creating, or generic and flat?

**Strong:** Title creates surprise through unexpected juxtaposition. Section headers use active verbs that propel the reader forward.
**Needs Work:** Title is clear but conventional. Headers describe content without creating momentum.
**Weak:** Generic title ("Introduction to X", "A Guide to Y"). Headers are just topic labels ("Background", "Discussion", "Conclusion").

**Common violations:**
- "A Comprehensive Guide to..."
- "Everything You Need to Know About..."
- Section headers like "Overview", "Introduction", "Conclusion"
- Headers that describe the container rather than the content

**Rewrite pattern:** For titles, combine two concepts not usually paired. For headers, replace nouns with verb phrases: "Background" → "Why X Breaks Under Pressure". "Conclusion" → "The One Change That Matters".
